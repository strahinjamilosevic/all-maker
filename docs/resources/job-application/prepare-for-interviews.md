title: Prepare for the interviews
description: How to prepare for each round of the interview process.
---

Getting to the interview stage means your application worked. Now the process becomes a different kind of challenge. Most hiring processes have multiple rounds, and each one is designed to evaluate something specific. Preparing for interviews in general is not enough—you need to prepare for each round on its own terms.

## Culture fit — HR screen

This is usually the first conversation, often 30 minutes with a recruiter or HR generalist. It is not a technical evaluation. It is a filter to check whether you are who your resume says you are and whether you would function in this environment.

What they are assessing:
- Communication skills and general professionalism
- Whether your career story makes sense and hangs together
- Your reasons for leaving your current or previous role
- What you are looking for and whether this role fits that

How to prepare:
- Know your own story cold. Be able to walk through your career history clearly and confidently, connecting the dots between roles.
- Have a clear, honest answer for why you are looking and why this company specifically.
- Research the company's values, culture, and how they describe themselves. Know enough to have a real conversation.
- Avoid being negative about previous employers.

## Team fit — interview with future colleagues

This round gives the team you would join the chance to assess whether working with you day to day would be productive and comfortable. It is often more conversational than formal.

What they are assessing:
- How you think and collaborate
- Whether your working style is compatible with theirs
- How you handle disagreement, ambiguity, and feedback
- Whether they would actually want to sit next to you

How to prepare:
- Research who you are speaking with. Look at their LinkedIn. Understand what they work on.
- Prepare examples of how you have worked with different types of colleagues—engineers, product managers, designers, leadership.
- Think about times you disagreed with someone and how you handled it. This question comes up often.
- Have genuine questions ready about how the team operates, how decisions get made, and what the onboarding experience looks like.

## Technical assessment — written task or live evaluation

This round tests whether you can actually do the work. The format varies. Some companies send a written task to complete at home, followed by a review discussion. Others run a live session where you work through something together.

What they are assessing:
- The quality and clarity of your output
- Your process—how you think through a problem, not just the end result
- Your ability to receive and respond to feedback in real time

How to prepare for a written task:
- Read the brief carefully. Clarify anything ambiguous before you start.
- Treat it as real work. Apply the same standards you would on the job.
- Submit on time. How you handle the logistics is also observed.
- Be ready to explain every decision you made in the follow-up.

How to prepare for a live session:
- Think out loud. Interviewers want to see your process, not just your output.
- Ask clarifying questions before diving in. Assumptions made without checking are a signal.
- If you get stuck, say so and work through it visibly rather than going quiet.

## Leadership approval — final interview with a manager or executive

Late in the process, you will often meet someone in a leadership position who was not involved in the earlier rounds. Their job is to make the final call or to validate the team's recommendation.

What they are assessing:
- Strategic fit—whether your ambitions and trajectory align with where the company is going
- Maturity and judgment under pressure
- Whether you can engage at their level of abstraction

How to prepare:
- Think bigger. This conversation is less about your day-to-day tasks and more about impact, direction, and growth.
- Be ready to talk about where you want to be in a few years and how this role fits that trajectory.
- Have a perspective on the company's challenges and direction. You have done the research—use it.
- Prepare thoughtful questions about the company's strategy, priorities, and how your function fits into the larger picture.

## Negotiating the offer

An offer is not the end of the process. It is the start of a negotiation. Most candidates accept the first number they are given. That is almost always a mistake.

How to approach it:
- **Do not give a number first if you can avoid it.** When asked for your salary expectation, it is reasonable to say you would like to hear their range first.
- **Know your market value before the conversation.** Research what the role pays in this location and at this company size. Use LinkedIn Salary, Glassdoor, or conversations with peers.
- **Negotiate the total package, not just the base.** Consider equity, bonus structure, pension contributions, remote flexibility, professional development budget, and start date.
- **Counter in writing.** It creates a record and gives both sides time to think.
- **Be direct but not adversarial.** Negotiation is expected. Companies price in room to move. Asking confidently does not put the offer at risk.
- **Know your walk-away number.** Before you negotiate, decide what you actually need and what you are willing to accept. Having that clarity makes the conversation easier.
